User interview script — AI fitness app
~15 min total Phase 1 · ~5 min Phase 2 · ~10 min Gym members & non-members Casual tone throughout
Intro Opening
1–2 min
"Hey, thanks so much for taking the time — really appreciate it. So this is super casual, no right or wrong answers. I'm not pitching anything, I'm just trying to understand what life looks like for you when it comes to health and fitness. I'll probably ask you to tell me about specific things that've happened, so just be as honest as you like. Cool to get started?"
Phase 1 Problem discovery — does the pain exist?
~5 min
Goal: validate that friction exists between people and their fitness goals — without ever mentioning the product. Listen for unprompted frustration about guesswork, inconsistency, or not knowing what to do. If no real pain surfaces, skip Phase 2 and wrap up early.
Q1 · Opener
"So what are you most focused on in life right now — what's taking up most of your headspace?"
Starts completely broad. Reveals how fitness ranks against everything else in their life. If they don't mention it at all, that's a signal too.
Q2 · Fitness context
"And where does working out or staying active fit into all of that for you?"
Still not leading. Lets them frame fitness in their own words — obligation, passion, afterthought, struggle.
Q3 · Concrete past event
Key signal
"Think back to the last time you felt like your fitness routine wasn't quite working the way you wanted. What was going on?"
Anchors them to a real memory, not a hypothetical. Watch for mentions of confusion, inconsistency, or feeling lost — those map directly to your value prop.
If they go quiet, nudge:
"Walk me through what that actually looked like week to week."
"What was the most frustrating part of it?"
Q4 · Stakes & implications
Make or break
"What did that actually cost you — time, results, motivation? What were the knock-on effects?"
Tests whether this is a real pain or just mild inconvenience. If they shrug it off or struggle to answer, the problem may not be urgent enough to build on.
Q5 · Why it matters
"What makes you keep showing up anyway — like, what's actually driving it for you?"
Reveals intrinsic motivation. Strong emotional anchors here are a positive signal for engagement and retention.
Phase 2 Current solutions — how are they coping now?
~10 min
Goal: understand exactly what tools, habits, and workarounds they currently use — and where those break down. This is your competitive landscape and your feature brief rolled into one. Only enter Phase 2 if Phase 1 revealed genuine pain.
Q6 · Current approach
"So when that happened — how did you actually deal with it? What did you turn to?"
Maps their existing solution. Apps, coaches, YouTube, gym programs, nothing at all — all valuable data.
Follow-up probes:
"Talk me through exactly how you use that — like on a typical week."
"What does that actually look like when you sit down to plan a session?"
Q7 · Past alternatives
Probe deep
"Before you landed on that — what else had you tried? How did that go?"
Reveals switching behaviour. Multiple failed attempts = high motivation to find a better solution. One thing that mostly works = harder to displace.
Q8 · Friction points
Key signal
"What's the most annoying part of how you manage it now — the bit you wish you could just skip?"
This is your product brief. Unprompted mentions of "I never know what to do", "tracking is a pain", or "I just guess" are gold.
Q9 · Lifestyle factors
Key signal
"Has stuff like sleep, stress, or how busy life gets ever messed with your training? Tell me about a time that happened."
Tests awareness of the lifestyle-performance connection — the core hook of your product. If this lands naturally, they're a strong fit. If they look confused, it may need more education to sell.
Q10 · Commitment signal
Make or break
"Have you ever paid for something — a coach, an app, a plan — to help sort this out? What happened with that?"
Past spending is the strongest proxy for willingness to pay. "Yes, and I'd do it again" is a green light. "No, I just figure it out myself" is a real risk to the business model.
Q11 · Wrap-up
"Is there anything about this whole side of your life — the fitness, the planning, staying consistent — that you feel like nobody's really nailed yet?"
Open invitation for them to describe your product unprompted. If they describe something that sounds exactly like what you're building, you've found a real believer.
Interviewer reminders
Silence is your friend — count to 5 before filling a pause. People add the most important things after a gap.
If they drift into opinions about the future, gently redirect: "That's interesting — has anything like that come up for you before?"
Skip Phase 2 entirely if Phase 1 reveals no real pain. A 7-minute interview with a clear "no problem" signal is still valuable data.
Take notes on exact words and phrases — don't paraphrase in your notes. Their language is your copywriting.
Never say "our app" or hint at what you're building. You're here to learn, not pitch.
For non-gym members, swap "routine" for "staying active" in questions — keeps things accessible.
How many interviews to run
Minimum 10–15 interviews split across segments — at least 5 gym members and 5 non-members before drawing conclusions.
The goal isn't statistical significance — it's pattern saturation. You're done when strangers start repeating each other's exact pain points. That typically happens between interview 8 and 15.
Run in batches of 5, then pause and debrief before continuing.
Batch Segment Action after
1 5 gym members Debrief — check for signal
2 5 non-members Debrief — compare patterns
3+ Either / both Continue if signals are mixed or unclear

Red flag to watch for: If the interviewee never mentions frustration with guesswork, planning, or inconsistency unprompted — and shows no history of paying for fitness help — treat this as a negative signal, regardless of how enthusiastic they seem about the idea in general.
